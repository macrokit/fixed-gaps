# Fixed Gaps

*An essay on relational invariants — the things between us that never change.*

## 1. The observation

Between any two people, certain quantities are frozen at the moment they first
become defined, and no amount of time, effort, or reinterpretation can alter
them. If someone is twenty years older than you, they are twenty years older
than you forever. If someone is your sister, she is your sister forever. These
are not properties of either person alone; they are properties of the *pair*
— and unlike almost everything else about a pair of people, they do not move.

Call these **fixed gaps**: relations between two entities that are invariant
over time.

## 2. The gap is fixed; its meaning is not

A twenty-year age gap is an unbridgeable chasm between a five-year-old and a
twenty-five-year-old. Between a fifty-year-old and a seventy-year-old, it is
nearly nothing. The absolute difference never changed — but the *ratio* of
the two ages shrinks toward one as both grow.

This single arithmetic fact explains a great deal of family life. Parents and
children can eventually become something like friends not because the gap
closed, but because the proportion did. What feels like "growing closer" to an
older relative is often just this ratio effect playing out on schedule.

The general principle: **the invariant is the substrate; the meaning is a
non-invariant function of it.** What changes over a lifetime is never the gap
itself, only the weight and reading we give it.

## 3. Fixed relations remove negotiation

Friendships, marriages, and colleagueships are chosen — and therefore
revocable, and therefore in need of maintenance. Blood relations and age gaps
are given, not chosen. That is why they can bear weight that chosen
relationships cannot: you cannot be fired as a brother. It is also why they
can trap: there is no exit clause, only distance.

And because the structural facts between two people are frozen, everything
that *does* change — trust, resentment, intimacy — must live entirely in
interpretation and conduct. Family conflicts are rarely about the fixed facts;
they are fights over what the fixed facts should be taken to mean.

## 4. Positions rotate through a fixed lattice

The age structure of a family is rigid, so people do not change their
relations — they change their *location in* them. You do not stop being a
child; you additionally become a parent, then a grandparent. Much of what we
call maturing is discovering, from the other side, what a relation you have
been inside your whole life actually felt like to the other person.

## 5. Beyond people: gaps between objects

The pattern is not human at all. Any two things that came into existence carry
a fixed formation gap forever — two stars, two rocks, two documents, two
blocks in a chain. Geology and archaeology are built on this: strata do not
give absolute dates directly; they give *order* and *intervals*, and the
intervals are incorruptible. Causal order is stronger still: if A caused B, no
reinterpretation ever reverses it.

## 6. In physics, often only the gaps exist

This is the deepest version of the observation. Potential energy has no
absolute value — only energy *differences* are physical. Voltage is a
potential *difference*; the zero point is a convention. Position has no
absolute meaning; only distances and relative velocities are measurable.

So it is not merely that gaps between objects stay fixed while the objects
change. For many physical quantities, **the gap is the only measurable
reality**, and the "absolute value" was a fiction penciled in for convenience.
(The theory document makes this precise: every gap structure admits an
absolute-value assignment, but only up to an arbitrary constant — the gauge
freedom is exactly the fictional part.)

## 7. The relativity caveat — and why it strengthens the thesis

Einstein showed that some gaps we assumed were fixed are not: simultaneity,
elapsed time, and length all depend on the observer. But physics responded by
finding the *truly* invariant gaps underneath — the spacetime interval,
proper time, causal order. When one inventory of fixed gaps dissolved, the
framework was rebuilt around a deeper inventory. The instinct that reality is
anchored in invariant relations survived; only the list of which relations
qualify was revised.

The lesson generalizes: **which gaps are fixed depends on which
transformations you admit.** Enlarge the group of admissible transformations
and fewer gaps survive — but the survivors are more fundamental. This is
Felix Klein's Erlangen program applied to pairs of things.

## 8. The philosophical payoff: structuralism

If the changeable stuff (states, appearances, interpretations) is the surface,
and the fixed gaps are the skeleton, one is led to a position philosophers
call structuralism or relationalism — Leibniz's side of his dispute with
Newton. Objects may not have intrinsic, standalone natures at all; a thing may
be nothing more than the bundle of its fixed relations to everything else.

The theory document turns this into a small theorem: the complete table of
gaps determines the entire configuration of objects uniquely, up to a single
global translation — that is, up to precisely the information (an absolute
origin) that was never real in the first place.

## 9. Compressed

- Between people and between objects alike, what persists are **relations,
  not conditions**.
- The more fundamental the domain, the more the relations turn out to be
  **all there is**.
- What we experience as change is the drift of non-invariant readings over an
  invariant substrate — ratios shrinking, positions rotating through a fixed
  lattice, meanings renegotiated over facts that cannot be.
